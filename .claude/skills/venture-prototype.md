# /venture-prototype

Guide a founder through building the Minimum Viable Venture (MVV) — taking the verified design off paper and turning it into an actual business that is ready to start pilot sales.

**The distinction that matters:** Venture Prototyping is not selling. It is systematising. The goal is a business that looks and feels like the final product from the customer's standpoint — not a beta, not a test. When prototyping ends, you start sales. Not before.

**What you're building:** The MVV has five components, all of which must be in place before the first sale. They are developed in parallel, not in sequence. Progress on one does not wait for another.

## Pre-Work: Before Building Starts

### Team Roles

**Teach this:**
> "Three functional roles need to exist before prototyping begins. They don't have to be three separate people — in an early venture, the founder often covers more than one. But each responsibility must sit with a named person. If no one owns it, it won't get done."

**The three roles:**

1. **Field Lead** — owns delivery and operations. Responsible for the day-to-day running of the prototyping work: building the value chain, managing suppliers and partners, overseeing the customer journey in practice. Needs start-up execution experience, not corporate management experience.

2. **Business Support: Accounting** — owns the numbers. Tracks actual costs against the model, maintains the cash account, updates the financial model as real data comes in. Does not need to be a full accountant — but needs to understand small business financials.

3. **Business Support: Sales** — owns customer acquisition. Responsible for developing the sales approach, training salespeople, and building the initial pipeline. Needs hands-on sales experience at the level you're selling (end-consumer or business-to-business, depending on the venture).

**Ask for each role:**
> "Who is doing this? Name the person. If it's you, say so — but be honest about what that means for your bandwidth."

**Gate:** All three roles named and assigned.

Save to `05-Ventures/[Venture Name]/Prototype/00-Team.md`.

---

### Project Management Infrastructure

**Teach this:**
> "A venture prototype is a small business being built from scratch under time pressure. Without a project management structure in place, the work will drift — important things won't get tracked, money will be unaccounted for, and the team won't know what success looks like at each stage. This isn't bureaucracy. It's the minimum scaffolding that keeps a start-up from collapsing under its own ambiguity."

**Build the following:**

1. **Project statement** — mission in one sentence, timeline, key deliverables, and KPIs for the prototyping phase
2. **Org chart** — lines of reporting and communication, who is responsible for what resources
3. **Start-up work plan** — key outputs and milestones with target dates
4. **Reporting plan** — what data will be captured in the field, how often, by whom, in what format
5. **Cash account** — how funds will be held, disbursed, and accounted for during the prototype phase

**Gate:** All five elements documented and agreed by the team.

Save to `05-Ventures/[Venture Name]/Prototype/00-PM-Infrastructure.md`.

## The MVV: Five Components in Parallel

All five components are built simultaneously. Progress is tracked across all five — not sequentially. At any point, ask which components are furthest behind and prioritise accordingly.

---

### Component 1: Customer Journey

**Teach this:**
> "The Customer Journey is the complete experience of the product from the customer's standpoint — from the moment they first hear about it through to purchase, use, payment, and return. The product itself (the MVP) is a sub-component of this. You are not just designing what the product does. You are designing what it feels like to be a customer."

**Key decisions that must be made here — and cannot be significantly changed once the pilot starts:**
- Product design and configuration
- Delivery mechanism
- Pricing and payment structure
- After-sales service
- First-use experience

**Build method:**
- **Co-creation** — involve actual target customers in shaping the product and delivery. Not focus groups. Active design sessions.
- **Rapid prototyping** — build rough versions quickly, test with small groups, iterate. The goal is learning, not polish.
- **Simulation** — before going live, simulate the full customer journey end-to-end. Walk through it as if you are the customer.

**Contemplative prompt:**
> "Put yourself in the customer's position at the moment before they say yes. What do they need to feel — not think, feel — to take that step?"

**Gate:** All key customer journey decisions made. Full journey simulated end-to-end. Looks and feels like the final product from the customer's standpoint — not a beta.

Save to `05-Ventures/[Venture Name]/Prototype/01-Customer-Journey.md`.

### Component 2: Brand

**Teach this:**
> "Brand is not a logo. It is the promise your venture makes and the consistent way it shows up — in what it says, how it looks, and what it feels like to interact with. In a new market, brand does work that advertising can't: it signals that this is real, that it can be trusted, that it is for people like me."

**Build:**
1. **Brand positioning** — one sentence: who is it for, what does it do for them, why is it different from anything else they know
2. **Name** — tested with target customers. Does it mean what you think it means in their context?
3. **Visual identity** — enough to look professional at point of sale and in all customer-facing materials
4. **Sales collateral** — what does a salesperson hand over, show, or leave behind?
5. **Sales pitches** — scripted and tested. How do you introduce the product? Handle the three types of doubt (need, efficacy, capability)?

**Gate:** Positioning clear, name tested, collateral and pitches ready for sales.

Save to `05-Ventures/[Venture Name]/Prototype/02-Brand.md`.

---

### Component 3: Value Chain

**Teach this:**
> "The value chain is everything behind the customer experience — how the product gets made, how it gets to the customer, how money flows back. The rule during prototyping is: minimum capital expenditure, maximum flexibility. You are not building the steady-state operation. You are building the smallest version that can demonstrate the model works."

**Design principles:**
- Use contractors and short-term agreements, not permanent hires and long-term contracts
- Mimic the systems of any existing business you're embedded in — don't build parallel infrastructure when you can use what's there
- Simulate operations before committing. Walk through production, distribution, and back-office steps manually before automating

**Build:**
1. **Supplier map** — who provides what, at what cost, on what terms
2. **Production / delivery process** — step by step, from order to delivery
3. **Distribution and inventory** — how does the product get to the customer? What needs to be held where?
4. **Back-office processing** — payments, invoicing, record-keeping

**Gate:** All value chain steps mapped, simulated, and operable with minimum fixed costs.

Save to `05-Ventures/[Venture Name]/Prototype/03-Value-Chain.md`.

---

### Component 4: Management Systems

**Teach this:**
> "Management systems are how you know whether the business is working. During the pilot, you will be tracking operational and financial performance against the targets set in the design phase. Those targets only mean something if you have systems in place to measure against them — before sales start."

**Build:**
1. **KPI dashboard** — the handful of operational indicators that, if on track, mean the business is on track. Examples: conversion rate, cost per acquisition, units delivered per day, gross margin per unit.
2. **Financial tracking** — monthly P&L and cash flow. Structured to match the financial model from the design phase — not a new format.
3. **Reporting cadence** — how often does the team report, in what format, to whom?
4. **Decision rules** — at what point does a metric trigger a review or a change? Don't wait until the end of the pilot to find out something broke in month two.

**Gate:** Dashboard built, financial tracking in place, reporting cadence agreed, decision rules defined.

Save to `05-Ventures/[Venture Name]/Prototype/04-Management-Systems.md`.

---

### Component 5: HR

**Teach this:**
> "The people you hire during prototyping are on short-term contracts. You are hiring to learn — what does this role actually require? — as much as to deliver. Hire people who can handle ambiguity. Train them rigorously. If they're not working, replace them quickly. The pilot depends on having the right people in the right roles."

**Build:**
1. **Salespeople** — hired and trained before the pilot starts. How many? What profile? What does good look like for this specific venture?
2. **Sales simulations** — run before the pilot launches. Not roleplay for comfort. Pressure-tested simulations designed to surface what breaks.
3. **Onboarding process** — how does a new hire get up to speed quickly enough to be effective in the field?
4. **Performance standards** — what does success look like for each role, and how is it measured?

**Gate:** Salespeople hired and trained, simulations completed, performance standards defined.

Save to `05-Ventures/[Venture Name]/Prototype/05-HR.md`.

## Market Seeding

**Teach this:**
> "Market seeding is the opportunity to build your first wave of customers while you're still building the business. Done well, you arrive at launch day with a group of people who already understand the product, have helped shape it, and are ready to buy. Done poorly, you skip it — and launch into a cold market."

**Three seeding strategies:**

1. **Seed groups** — a cross-section of target customers recruited from the market. Their role is not to test the product. Their role is to become lead users: people who will buy early, talk about it, and bring others in.

2. **Co-creation workshops** — structured sessions where seed group members contribute to shaping the product. Their inputs are incorporated. This creates ownership, not just familiarity.

3. **Behaviour modelling workshops** — sessions structured so seed group participants practise the key behaviours the product requires. The goal: the product feels familiar, not foreign, by the time it launches.

**Gate:** Seed group recruited, at least one co-creation session completed, lead users identified and warm at time of launch.

Save to `05-Ventures/[Venture Name]/Prototype/06-Market-Seeding.md`.

## The Surrender Lens

At any stage, if the founder is:
- Treating prototyping as selling — pushing to start pilot before the MVV is ready → hold the gate
- Building one component and neglecting others — treating this as sequential → name the parallel requirement
- Over-engineering — building the steady-state operation rather than the minimum viable version → return to the design principle
- Skipping market seeding — treating it as optional → name what a cold launch costs
- Hiring permanent staff during prototyping → minimum cap-ex, maximum flexibility
- Avoiding the sales simulations because they're uncomfortable → name what an untrained sales team costs in the first week of the pilot

The MVV is not done when it feels ready. It is done when every gate is passed.
