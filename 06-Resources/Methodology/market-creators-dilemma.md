# The Market Creator's Dilemma — Notes

Source: Simanis & Donohue, MIT Sloan Management Review, October 2025

---

## The Core Dilemma

**Why do new markets fail so predictably?**

The conventional explanation blames execution: bad timing, weak teams, insufficient capital, wrong pricing. But the data doesn't support this. Most market-creation failures share a deeper structural problem — one that conventional innovation practice not only fails to address, but actively creates.

The true source of the problem: By defining product ideas based on what customers want and need — a proven principle of new product innovation — market creators unwittingly import a **faulty core business architecture (CBA)**.

---

## What Is a CBA?

The **Core Business Architecture** is the DNA of a market. It defines:
- The activities required to make, sell, and deliver the product
- The resource types those activities require
- The cost structure that results

Every product idea has an embedded CBA — the set of activities and resources implied by how the product is conceived. When you define a product by what it does for customers, you import the conventional CBA for that category.

**The trap:** The conventional CBA for most categories has a cost floor that sits above the value ceiling for the target customer segment. This is the **Cost-to-Value Barrier**.

---

## The Cost-to-Value Barrier

A faulty CBA produces a structural impossibility: the minimum achievable unit cost exceeds the maximum price customers will pay. No amount of operational efficiency can fix this — you can optimise execution of a fundamentally unworkable architecture, but you cannot make it viable.

**Faulty CBA formula:**
Conventional cost floor > Customer value ceiling → No viable market

**Working CBA formula:**
Engineered cost floor < Customer value ceiling → Market creation possible

---

## The Critical Limiting Operation (CLO)

Within any conventional BFF (Business Form Factor), there is one activity that:
1. Is central to delivering the core product promise, AND
2. Disproportionately drives up unit cost

This is the **Critical Limiting Operation**. It sets the cost floor.

**Examples from the paper:**

**BMW peer-to-peer car sharing:**
CLO = physical inspection and cleaning of vehicles between each rental
Cost of CLO per transaction made the required price unacceptable at the target segment's value level. The architecture was faulty from the start — BMW was importing the Hertz/Avis model into a low-cost peer-to-peer context.

**Fortified snack foods (emerging markets):**
CLO = cold chain logistics required to maintain nutritional stability
Conventional food distribution relied on cold chain — prohibitively expensive at rural BoP price points. The product idea imported a faulty CBA.

**Blue Apron / meal kit delivery:**
CLO = per-meal ingredient portioning and packing
Labour-intensive packing couldn't scale below a price customers wouldn't pay for home cooking convenience over restaurant alternatives.

**Rent the Runway:**
CLO = dry-cleaning and quality inspection per garment per rental
Per-use cost of cleaning and inspection created a floor above what fashion customers were willing to pay per outfit rental.

**Zebra Fuel (petrol delivery):**
CLO = mobile tanker + driver per delivery visit
Last-mile delivery cost per transaction was multiples of the margin available at pump prices.

## Where Faulty CBAs Come From

> "By defining product ideas based on what customers want and need — a proven principle of new product innovation — market creators unwittingly import a faulty core business architecture."

The innovation process that works for existing markets fails for new markets because:

1. **Existing markets have working CBAs.** Incumbents have already solved the cost-to-value problem through decades of optimisation. When you innovate within an existing market, you inherit a working architecture.

2. **New markets have no working CBA.** The target segment has never been served profitably. There is no existing architecture to inherit — only the conventional CBA for analogous product categories.

3. **Customer research points to the wrong design.** Customers describe needs in terms of products they already know. Their needs point straight toward the conventional CBA — because that's the frame they have. Following the customer into product design means following them into the faulty architecture.

---

## The Systems Engineering Solution

The paper argues that market creators need to apply **systems engineering** — the same discipline used in aerospace, defence, and civil infrastructure — to the design of the product idea itself.

Systems engineering defines three sequential processes:

### 1. Define Functions
What must the venture do to create and capture value?
Not features — functions. The minimum set of capabilities the system must perform.

### 2. Derive Requirements
What constraints must each function satisfy to produce a viable market?
Requirements are derived from the functions, not from customer preferences. They include cost ceilings, performance thresholds, and architectural constraints.

### 3. Design the Form Factor
What configuration of activities and resources satisfies all requirements simultaneously?
This is where "productizing" happens — reshaping the product idea so that delivering it creates synergies across requirements rather than conflicts.

**The key insight:** By engineering the product idea to satisfy requirements, you break the CLO. You don't just improve the conventional BFF — you replace it with an architecture that the conventional CBA never could have produced.

---

## Productizing 10 Requirements Across 3 Functions

The paper introduces (or references) a framework of **10 requirements across 3 functions**:

**F1 — Create Value Surplus** (R1-R3)
Make, sell, and deliver at unit cost below customer value ceiling

**F2 — Normalise Customer Routines** (R4-R7)
Get the product into widespread use without expensive behaviour change campaigns

**F3 — Dictate Competitive Landscape** (R8-R10)
Shape the competitive environment so the venture captures the surplus it creates

The productizing challenge is to design a single product architecture that satisfies all 10 requirements *simultaneously* — because they interact. A design that solves F1 but breaks F2 is not viable. Synergies between requirements are the source of durable competitive advantage.

---

## Implications for IVE/BALM Practice

1. **The CLO is the first thing to identify.** Before any other design work, ask: what is the single activity in the conventional BFF that makes this market unworkable? That is the design target.

2. **Customer research confirms pain; it doesn't design architecture.** Use R2 research to confirm the value bottleneck. Don't use it to determine the product form. Customers cannot see past the conventional CBA.

3. **Faulty CBA = design problem, not execution problem.** If the unit economics don't work at simulation, more capital or better execution won't fix it. Redesign the CBA.

4. **The BFF that breaks the CLO often looks wrong.** It doesn't resemble the conventional product. This is a feature, not a bug — that's the point. Resist the pull toward familiar form factors.

5. **Synergies across requirements are the moat.** A venture that satisfies all 10 requirements through a unified design is far harder to replicate than one that solves F1 alone. The moat is architectural, not operational.

---

## Surrender Lens

The Market Creator's Dilemma names the deepest form of entrepreneurial forcing: imposing a product form that follows naturally from customer research and conventional wisdom, without questioning whether the architecture of that product form makes the market viable.

The forced path: customer says X → build X → import faulty CBA → optimise execution → run out of capital → blame execution.

The responding path: customer says X → what function does X serve? → what are the requirements for that function to create value at scale? → what form factor satisfies those requirements? → build that, even if it doesn't look like X.

Surrender in venture design means letting the requirements shape the form, not the other way around.
