## Introduction: The Challenge and the Opportunity

### Why market-creating ventures fail

Market-creating innovations — those bringing new functionality into the world that solve previously unsolved problems — create transformational impact and enormous profit opportunities. Yet less than 1% achieve consistent profitability. Venture Genome calculates that 90% of startups fail completely; of those that produce a successful exit, less than 20% will become profitable.

The reason is not the complexity and uncertainty of the innovation challenge itself. It results from the innovation methods used today to tackle it.

The conventional approach — define a product idea based on a customer problem; fill in a business model canvas; launch and experiment until profitable — violates the science of innovating new complex systems under high uncertainty. Specifically:

1. It drives up a venture's underlying cost structure at the start, which significantly narrows (and potentially closes off completely) the path to profitability.
2. Experimenting in-market introduces enormous noise, making it nearly impossible to determine why a venture isn't working, what to change, or whether it is critically flawed.

Examples: Blue Apron accumulated $700M in losses before selling its assets. Hello Fresh, the market leader with $7B in sales, has never sustained profitability. Goldman Sachs's Marcus lost $5B in four years. Amazon's Grand Challenge Lab was shuttered in 2023.

**The fatal flaw:** Solving the product idea to optimise for the customer's needs alone, and then handing off to the business model the full burden of making, selling, delivering, and getting paid profitably. In systems engineering: "Optimising a part, pessimises the system."

## IVE and the V-Model: Current Mapping

**Important:** Different people use different versions of the V-model. Some have a simple three-level V (system → sub-system → component). Others have more levels. When using the V-model as an analogy with a founder or engineer, establish which version they know before mapping IVE onto it.

**What is confirmed:**

- **BAP (Business Architecture Plan) = top-left corner only.** The 6 functions and 13 requirements are the system-level requirements. They sit at the very top left of the V — not the whole left side. They are fixed because of high interdependency: how you solve R1 (cost) affects R3 (adoption), which affects R5 (lock-in), which affects R9 (competitor entry). The product idea must be solved holistically against all 13 simultaneously.

- **Middle-left and bottom-left mapping: still open.** How the product idea development, at-scale operational model, and financial model map onto the sub-system and component design levels of the V has not yet been finalised.

- **Right side of the V maps cleanly:**
  - Bottom-right (component build and test) = MVV component prototyping — individual components of the Minimum Viable Venture (Customer Journey, Brand, Value Chain, Management Systems, HR) built and tested independently
  - Middle-right (sub-system integration and test) = Full MVV — all five components integrated and operating together
  - Top-right (full system validation) = Pilot — minimum representative business unit tested in-market

- **The tradeable distinction:** Once at the right side of the V — business model components — these are tradeable and can be optimised without touching the core architecture. Sales scripts, supplier relationships, delivery mechanisms, pricing structures can all be iterated. The BAP requirements cannot.

## Chapter 1: The Relevance of Systems Engineering

### Systems Engineering's V-model

Systems engineering — the discipline used to innovate extremely complex systems under high uncertainty with remarkable reliability — is applied to market-creating ventures through IVE.

The V-model traces the shape of the letter V:
- **Left side (top-down):** Design, model, and verify the best possible solution on paper
- **Right side (bottom-up):** Build, test, and validate the solution in the quickest, least costly way

### The business architecture

The business architecture is the deep strategy or logic for how a venture works — like a DNA for a business. It:
- Constrains the venture's performance potential
- Sets its core cost structure
- Up to 70% of a venture's cost structure is "baked in" at the point the core idea is defined

**Business architecture vs. business model:**
- The *business architecture* is the deep strategy that makes it possible to profitably bring a new functionality to market. It defines the average cost curve for the entire industry.
- The *business model* is a company's strategy for deploying the business architecture in a way that outcompetes others. It governs each company's profitability within the architecture.

The way to end up with a good business architecture is to solve for the product idea **holistically** — taking into account all essential business requirements simultaneously. This creates architectural synergies. The goal is a tightly integrated architecture, not independent solutions to each requirement.

### Why simplicity is the goal

"What makes for a good core idea is simplicity. Elegant, simple solutions are more reliable because fewer things can fail. They also cost less, as there's less that goes into making them and less to manage, repair, and replace." — Steve Jobs's famous obsession.

The Apollo Lunar Orbit Rendezvous solution was selected precisely because it created synergies: a smaller lunar rover reduced fuel requirements, which reduced tank size, which eliminated the need for new more powerful rockets.

## Chapter 2: The Venture Lifecycle

IVE's V-model has two sides:

**Left side — Design and Verify (typically 6 months):**
Innovate a product idea and its underlying business architecture top-down on paper. Verify it is commercially robust using three modelling tools: a logic model, an at-scale operational model, and an at-scale financial model. Produces a business model blueprint at the bottom of the V.

**Right side — Build and Validate:**
Takes the business architecture and adds the detail needed to prototype, test, and validate it — working bottom-up from individual components up through assemblies and ultimately to a pilot of the minimum representative business unit (12–24 months).

**The business model blueprint** (bottom of the V) is the bridge. It lists all product, information, and money components from the product idea, along with:
- How each component must work (from the logic model)
- The performance metrics each must meet (from the at-scale financial model)
- The four types of business model components needed to make and manage each flow: people, processes, assets, and control systems

This is what enables bottom-up building and testing: by knowing how each component works in service of the overall system, each can be evaluated independently.

## Chapter 3: The Goal of Design and Verification

The Design and Verify stage culminates in five artefacts:

1. **Verified Product Idea** — a one-page written description that:
   - Elegantly brings together 13 essential strategies that drive commercial potential
   - Can credibly perform/sustain all business activities flowing from the product idea
   - Has a credible cost range at least 25% lower than the credible price range paid by customers

2. **Business Architecture (Logic Model / BAP)** — defines six core business functions and their 13 essential requirements that dictate commercial potential, regardless of industry

3. **At-Scale Operational Model** — visual map of product, information, and money flows at maturity, broken into operating unit levels

4. **At-Scale Financial Model** — translates the operational model into numbers; generates required price vs. target price

5. **Business Model Engineering Plan (Venture Breakdown)** — detailed "parts list" of everything that makes up the business, with performance parameters from the financial model and design constraints from the logic model

## Chapter 4: Defining the Prime Impact Opportunity (PIO)

The Prime Impact Opportunity is the broad application for the core functionality shared by a group of people or organisations that holds the greatest value potential to achieve the venture's investment requirements.

### When starting with a technology

Four steps:

**Step 1: Define Core Functionality**
What the technology does (utility) and how it does it (form) at its most basic level. State with the least resolution needed to identify use cases — strips away premature design elements.

*Example: A high-altitude kite generating electricity → core functionality: "70 kilowatts of energy generated through a truck-sized airborne kite flown at high altitude."*

**Step 2: Define Investment Requirements**
Four inputs:
- Investment period (timeframe for return on capital)
- Required IRR (annualised ROI expected by investors)
- At-scale geography (territory to be served by end of investment period)
- Relevant industry (constraints the solution must belong to)

These inputs generate a rough estimate of required at-scale revenue, and — divided by a credible customer base — a rough required price. This becomes the sense-check for use cases.

**Step 3: Identify Use Cases**
Broad applications of the core functionality shared by a group of people or organisations. Probe business-to-consumer, business-to-business, and business-to-government applications.

**Step 4: Evaluate Use Cases and Select the PIO**
Compare required price range across use cases against the urgency and importance of each. Select the use case where the required price is most credible given the size of the loss being solved.

### When starting with a social challenge

Same four steps, with adapted language:
- Step 1: Define the Pressing Societal Issue (at a broad enough level to expand opportunity size)
- Step 3: Identify Opportunity Cases (industry-relevant causes or consequences of the societal issue)

## Chapter 5: Introduction to the Logic Model

The Business Architecture Plan (BAP) is a logic model — a description of the key chain of causes and effects that explains how a complex system works.

It defines **six core business functions** and their **thirteen essential requirements** that dictate commercial potential regardless of industry — arrived at by mining business research for first principles, testing them in field, and isolating the most essential.

The BAP also contains the chain of research inputs needed to arrive at a theory of change (the scientific explanation of what a solution has to do) and to evaluate potential strategies.

**The goal:** Productize all strategies in the simplest way — bake them into the product idea itself as synergistically as possible. The product idea gains greater robustness and resolution with every strategy productized, like a marble statue being carved.

**A theory of change is required for each strategy** — a credible logical chain explaining why and how it works. Analogues from other industries are commonly used.

## Chapter 6: Basic Commercial Viability (Stage I of the BAP)

Stage I contains three functions: Create Surplus Value, Exchange Surplus Value, and Retain Surplus Value. These are the minimum set needed for basic commercial viability. The output is the "essential product idea."

---

### Function 1: Create Surplus Value

A solution that creates more core value for customers than it costs to make, sell, and deliver.

Two requirements work in tandem:

#### Requirement 1: Circumvent the Cost Bottleneck

**The concept:** Every conventional business model for addressing a problem has a Critical Limiting Activity — one activity that disproportionately drives up costs and makes it difficult to solve the problem affordably. The product idea must be designed to circumvent this activity entirely — not optimise it.

**Process:**
1. Map the Conventional Business Model — how would a venture conventionally provide the core functionality to the Prime Opportunity Case?
2. Identify the Critical Limiting Activity — the activity contributing the greatest proportion of costs
3. Source analogues from other industries where this activity has been eliminated
4. Define a Workaround Theory of Change — a credible logical chain explaining why elimination works

*Example: Conventional lending to unbanked people requires hours of home visits to assess creditworthiness — the Critical Limiting Activity. Micro-finance's workaround: harness peer pressure and peer support (joint liability) to change repayment behaviour, eliminating the need for individual credit assessment. Default rates at Compartamos: typically less than 2%.*

#### Requirement 2: Eliminate the Key Monetizable Customer Loss

**The concept:** The biggest unnecessary loss in customers' current routine for achieving an important outcome. The more important the outcome and the bigger the loss, the greater the value created and the higher the credible price.

**Three types of loss:**
- **Money** — the product saves or generates money. Most objective; easiest to monetise.
- **Fear** — eliminates the fear of not accomplishing an important outcome. Requires comparables to establish monetisable value.
- **Stress** — eliminates the stress of managing the current routine. Least robust — customers acclimate to routines and often don't perceive the stress.

**Important distinctions:**
- A Customer Loss is not the same as a Customer Pain Point. Not all pain points harbour unnecessary costs. If there is no unnecessary cost eliminated, there is nothing that can be credibly monetised.
- Behind every successful gain point there is a Customer Loss — and the monetisable value of the gain point is determined by the size of that loss.

**Sizing the loss:**
For a money loss: calculate the total cost eliminated from the customer's current routine over the life of the product.
*Example: Indoor solar lighting reduces kerosene use by 80%. If customers spend $5/month and the product lasts 60 months: 80% × $5 × 60 months = $240 in savings.*

**Key guardrail:** The whole cost of the solution must be lower than the size of the Customer Loss eliminated — otherwise no surplus value is being created. This is a reference point for all downstream financial testing.

**Interim financial check after R1 and R2:**
Sense-check the draft product idea against: (a) rough variable costs to create an offering with required performance, (b) rough price to end user given variable costs, (c) resulting at-scale revenue from the Prime Opportunity Case.

---

### Function 2: Exchange Surplus Value

Convincing customers to pay more for the solution than it costs the venture. Two requirements:

#### Requirement 3: Circumvent the Customer Adoption Bottleneck

**The concept:** New-to-world solutions pose a unique adoption challenge — the value they create and the routines involved aren't already normalised in customers' lives. They aren't conditioned to see the value or to budget for it.

Three types of adoption block:
- **Want block** — customers are not aware of the Key Loss
- **Buy block** — customers do not understand the source or cause of the Key Loss, or doubt the product works
- **Use block** — the product use routine significantly disrupts current routines or requires significant learning

**Process:** Map the Interim Product Use Routine. Identify the most significant Want/Buy/Use Block. Source intervention strategies from other industries. Define an Adoption Theory of Change.

*Example: In a Chinese English-learning venture, parents faced a buy block — they wouldn't recognise that the learning design was superior to popular apps without knowing language acquisition theory. The adoption strategy: make it part of the curriculum used by teachers in class, harnessing the automatic credibility of trusted experts.*

#### Requirement 4: Inhibit Customer Journey Friction

**The concept:** Identify the Primary Want/Buy/Use Trigger — the general context where customers presently engage with information and solutions relevant to the outcome being addressed. Translate key triggers into People, Place, and Partner components of the operational model.

*Example: In a fintech venture helping people invest savings, research revealed that employers were already the context where employees engaged with financial security topics (paychecks, retirement plans, tax). The product idea was reconceptualised as an employer benefits programme for financial resilience.*

**Interim financial check after R3 and R4:**
Sense-check variable costs, rough end-user price, and resulting at-scale revenue at higher fidelity. Add a rough estimate of customer acquisition cost as a share of estimated gross margin.

---

### Function 3: Retain Surplus Value

Holding on to enough money to generate a competitive return on capital. Two requirements:

#### Requirement 5: Block Customer Flight

**The concept:** Harness a Key Value Switching Cost — a step in the product use routine where the greatest amount of value can be stored up and made subject to loss upon switching to a competitor.

**Three types of switching cost:**
- **Money** — switching will cost extra money
- **Fear** — switching may compromise achieving the outcome
- **Stress** — switching will come with extra hassle

*Example: In a gamified nutrition venture for children, daily prizes became inputs to a progressive game structure laddering up to a final prize — which would be lost if the customer switched brands.*

**Process:** Define the Interim Customer Journey. Identify where the Key Switching Cost can be created. Define the Lock-In Theory of Change.

#### Requirement 6: Block Competitor Entry

**The concept:** Identify and protect the Key Inimitable Resource — the asset central to delivering the core value proposition for which there is limited supply and few alternatives.

**Process:** Define the Required Core Competency. Decompose it into underlying resources. Identify the resource that can be most credibly insulated from replication. Define the strategy for sustaining its inimitability.

*Example: In a shared mobility venture matching co-leasers of vehicles, the ability to match people depended on a large base of university students (different transport schedules from full-time workers). Lock-out strategy: build trusted relationships with academic institutions. Product reconceptualised as a university-backed programme to help students persist through college.*

**Interim financial check after R5 and R6:**
Sense-check at higher fidelity. Add a rough estimate of cost of working capital as a share of estimated gross margin.

## Chapter 7: The Need for a New Approach to Modelling

Standard financial models for new market ventures have "high resolution but low fidelity" — detailed numbers, but disconnected from the fundamental commercial logic.

**The core problem:** Commercial fundamentals aren't individual line items on a P&L or individual cells on a spreadsheet. They cut across a venture — the logic for acquiring customers manifests simultaneously in target market segmentation, product design, go-to-market channels, and HR cost structure. In the quest to generate five-year P&Ls built on hundreds of variables, the forest is lost for the trees.

**The systems engineering approach:** Build a model that represents the core solution's architecture with the least amount of resolution. Use it to run simulations to understand how the architecture responds to change and stress. Simulation builds robustness into the core architecture.

**The process:**
1. Logic model (BAP) — explains how the venture needs to work, with financial checks throughout
2. At-scale operational model — details the flows of product, money, and information required for the venture to work as specified
3. At-scale financial model — calculates required price for profitability using the activity drivers and HR positions from the operational model

**Measuring robustness:** The margin of safety (headroom) between required price and credible price. Ideally, the required price is less than 75% of the credible price — because 70–75% of a system's whole cost structure is locked in once the core architecture is defined. For the design stage to be meaningful, this margin must exist on paper.

*Example from the textbook's legal tech case: Credible price range £400–£750. Required mean price £270, best-case £160, worst-case £335. The venture had room to absorb real-world variability.*

## Chapter 8: At-Scale Operational Modelling

The operational model decomposes the product idea into three categories of flows:

1. **Product flows** — the product components going to the end user
2. **Information flows** — critical information moving among people and places (marketing and production information)
3. **Money flows** — money transactions taking place among people and places

Every person and resource in a venture can be traced to an activity involved in executing, managing, or supporting one of these three flows.

The model is also **unitised** — it breaks the venture into its operating unit levels, starting with the Last-Mile Unit serving the end customer and working backward to the head office.

### Four-step modelling process

**Step 1: Identify Key People and Places**

*People* on the customer side fall into three groups:
- **Customers** — people who pay for the product
- **Users** — people who use the product (may differ from customers, e.g. students vs. schools)
- **Enablers** — people whose buy-in is required by the customer or user (e.g. parents, teachers)

*Places* are spaces or institutional sites involved in making, selling, marketing, or using the product. Can be physical or virtual. Goal: identify the key places impacting how the venture manages its key flows.

**Step 2: List the three flows**
For each flow, list: product components going to the end user; critical information moving among people and places; money transactions.

**Step 3: Map flows from head office to end user**
Trace the movement of product, information, and money among the identified people and places. Bracket the operating unit levels (Last-Mile Unit, Territory, Head Office).

**Step 4: Identify activities and staff**
Determine the range of business activities needed to support the flows, and the staff required. These are the key inputs to the at-scale financial model.

*Recommended tools: Lucid Chart (can export data objects as CSV for use in financial model); Miro. Organise flows into layers with toggle on/off for complex models.*
